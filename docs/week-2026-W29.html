The contamination of aquaculture products and effluents by Emerging Contaminants (ECs)—originating from both direct chemical applications and peripheral industrial activities—represents a critical global concern due to the environmental and public health risks associated with their persistence, bioaccumulation, and environmental dispersion. This systematic review aimed to synthesize the available scientific evidence on the occurrence, sources, environmental and human health impacts, and mitigation strategies of ECs in aquaculture production systems. A systematic literature search was conducted following the PRISMA 2020 guidelines across the Web of Science, Scopus, and Google Scholar databases. Studies were selected according to predefined eligibility criteria, resulting in a total of 137 studies included in this review. Given their environmental persistence and ubiquitous distribution, there is an urgent need for advanced analytical methodologies, robust regulatory frameworks, and the implementation of sustainable treatment technologies—such as advanced oxidation processes and recirculating aquaculture systems—alongside strategies for reducing antibiotics and microplastics. These measures are essential to mitigate the prevalence of ECs and safeguard both ecosystem integrity and human health.

ABSTRACT Rice and fish are staple foods essential for food security, supplying carbohydrates, proteins, amino acids and fatty acids vital to human nutrition. In Vietnam, they also underpin local and national economies. Rice is predominantly cultivated in irrigated systems under diverse ecological and hydrological conditions, particularly in flood‐prone areas that sustain wild fish habitats, aquatic biodiversity and ecosystem productivity. Global Burden of Clinostomum complanatum in Freshwater Fish: A Systematic Review and Meta‐Analysis of Prevalence and Pathological Effect
👤 Getnet, Mengesha Ayehu; Fenta, Melkie Dagnaw; Berihun, Asnak... 📅 2026-07-13
ABSTRACT Clinostomum complanatum infection is a trematode parasite infecting diverse freshwater fish hosts, causing pathological damage and economic losses. Despite its veterinary and public health importance, its global burden remains insufficiently quantified. This study aimed to assess the global burden of C. complanatum infection in fish, focusing on prevalence, habitat associations and pathological impacts. A systematic review and meta‐analysis were conducted according to Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta‐Analyses (PRISMA) 2020 guidelines. Literature published from database inception to 7 June 2025 was searched. Thirty studies involving 70 freshwater fish species from 18 families were included. Pooled prevalence estimates were calculated using random‐effects models in R software. Heterogeneity was assessed using Cochran's Q and I 2 statistics, whereas subgroup analyses and Egger's regression test were performed to evaluate variability and publication bias. The prevalence of C. complanatum varied markedly among fish species, reaching 100% in Squalidus chankaensis but remaining undetected in species such as Cobitis biwae , Hypomesus transpacificus , Salangichthys microdon and Silurus asotus . The pooled global prevalence was 28.7% (95% confidence interval [CI]: 22.0%–36.5%), with substantial heterogeneity ( I 2 = 99.0%). Significant variation was observed across fish families, geographic regions and habitat types. The highest prevalence occurred in Percidae (97%), Osphronemidae (67%) and Gobionidae (65%). Regionally, Africa (49%) and Europe (46%) showed the highest prevalence. Infection rates were higher in aquaculture systems (45%) than in natural habitats. Metacercariae predominantly encyst in muscle, buccal and branchial tissues, producing yellow grub cysts associated with muscle degeneration and inflammatory responses. The internal transcribed spacer (ITS) region was the most frequently used molecular marker, although molecular diagnostic approaches remained limited overall. C. complanatum is a widespread and heterogeneous threat to freshwater fish health. Standardized surveillance, greater use of molecular diagnostics and targeted control strategies, particularly in aquaculture systems, are essential to reduce infection burden, economic losses and potential zoonotic risks.

👤 Soibelzon, Leopoldo Hector; Robbins, Alexandra Mary Claire 📅 2026-07-13
Direct observations of predator–prey interactions are essential for understanding the foraging ecology of apex predators, yet such records remain rare in Antarctic marine systems. Leopard seals (Hydrurga leptonyx) are generalist predators known to consume prey across multiple trophic levels, but most knowledge of their fish consumption derives from indirect methods that provide limited behavioural context. Herein, we report a detailed observation of a leopard seal actively hunting and consuming a notothenid fish in the shallow coastal waters of the western Antarctic Peninsula. On 2 January 2026, a single adult individual was observed in the Gerlache Strait for approximately 30 minutes performing repeated near-bottom dives in waters less than 3 m deep. Following this extended searching phase, the leopard seal surfaced holding a live black rockcod (Notothenia coriiceps) and engulfed it whole within seconds, without visible prey processing or dismemberment. The complete behavioural sequence, including benthic searching and subsequent prey capture, was observed from the surface, while prey handling and ingestion at the surface were documented using high-resolution photography. This observation provides rare direct evidence of active benthic foraging and fish predation in a nearshore Antarctic environment. In contrast to previous accounts describing partial processing of notothenid prey prior to ingestion, the present record documents the consumption of a relatively large fish without external handling, suggesting greater flexibility in prey-handling strategies than previously recognised. The prolonged searching behaviour further supports the role of leopard seals as active benthic hunters in shallow coastal habitats. These findings highlight the value of direct behavioural observations for interpreting predator ecology and contribute to a more nuanced understanding of foraging strategies in a rapidly changing Antarctic ecosystem.
