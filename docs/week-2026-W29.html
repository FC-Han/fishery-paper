Global Burden of Clinostomum complanatum in Freshwater Fish: A Systematic Review and Meta‐Analysis of Prevalence and Pathological Effect
👤 Getnet, Mengesha Ayehu; Fenta, Melkie Dagnaw; Berihun, Asnak... 📅 2026-07-13
ABSTRACT Clinostomum complanatum infection is a trematode parasite infecting diverse freshwater fish hosts, causing pathological damage and economic losses. Despite its veterinary and public health importance, its global burden remains insufficiently quantified. This study aimed to assess the global burden of C. complanatum infection in fish, focusing on prevalence, habitat associations and pathological impacts. A systematic review and meta‐analysis were conducted according to Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta‐Analyses (PRISMA) 2020 guidelines. Literature published from database inception to 7 June 2025 was searched. Thirty studies involving 70 freshwater fish species from 18 families were included. Pooled prevalence estimates were calculated using random‐effects models in R software. Heterogeneity was assessed using Cochran's Q and I 2 statistics, whereas subgroup analyses and Egger's regression test were performed to evaluate variability and publication bias. The prevalence of C. complanatum varied markedly among fish species, reaching 100% in Squalidus chankaensis but remaining undetected in species such as Cobitis biwae , Hypomesus transpacificus , Salangichthys microdon and Silurus asotus . The pooled global prevalence was 28.7% (95% confidence interval [CI]: 22.0%–36.5%), with substantial heterogeneity ( I 2 = 99.0%). Significant variation was observed across fish families, geographic regions and habitat types. The highest prevalence occurred in Percidae (97%), Osphronemidae (67%) and Gobionidae (65%). Regionally, Africa (49%) and Europe (46%) showed the highest prevalence. Infection rates were higher in aquaculture systems (45%) than in natural habitats. Metacercariae predominantly encyst in muscle, buccal and branchial tissues, producing yellow grub cysts associated with muscle degeneration and inflammatory responses. The internal transcribed spacer (ITS) region was the most frequently used molecular marker, although molecular diagnostic approaches remained limited overall. C. complanatum is a widespread and heterogeneous threat to freshwater fish health. Standardized surveillance, greater use of molecular diagnostics and targeted control strategies, particularly in aquaculture systems, are essential to reduce infection burden, economic losses and potential zoonotic risks.

👤 Shubham; Yadav, Ajay Kumar; Maurya, Pradip Kumar; Kumar, Din... 📅 2026-07-14
This study evaluated fish silage as an alternative protein source for replacing fish meal in the diet of pacu fingerlings (Piaractus brachypomus). A 90-day feeding trial involving fifteen experimental tanks was conducted to rear pacu fingerlings. These tanks were assigned to five dietary treatments, with three replicates for each treatment. The treatments were: Control (100% fish meal), T1 (25% replacement of fish meal protein with fish silage), T2 (50% replacement), T3 (75% replacement), and T4 (100% replacement). Fifteen healthy fingerlings were introduced into each aerated tank and fed the experimental diets twice daily at a rate of 5% of their body weight throughout the experiment. The prepared fish silage achieved a pH level of 3.5 after a 30-day ensiling period, indicating its effective preservation and suitability as an ingredient for aquafeed. Water quality parameters such as temperature, pH, dissolved oxygen, total dissolved solids (TDS), total ammonia and nitrite were monitored weekly and no significant differences (p>0.05) were observed among the various groups. The growth parameters evaluated were: body length, weight, percentage weight gain (WG %), specific growth rate (SGR), feed conversion ratio (FCR), protein efficiency ratio (PER), and survival rate. The results revealed significant (p<0.05) differences in growth and survival among the various dietary treatments. The experiment concluded that fish silage can replace up to 50% of fish meal protein in the diet of P. brachypomus fingerlings without adversely affecting growth performance.

Occurrence, Bycatch, and Fishery Interactions of the Vulnerable Common Smooth-hound Shark (Mustelus mustelus Linnaeus, 1758) in the Mediterranean Waters off the Gaza Strip, Palestine
👤 Rabou, Abdel Fattah N. Abd 📅 2026-07-14
The Common Smooth-hound Shark (Mustelus mustelus Linnaeus, 1758) is a widespread Mediterranean elasmobranch frequently impacted by artisanal fisheries. This study investigates its occurrence, bycatch patterns, habitat associations, and fishery interactions in the Gaza Strip, Palestine, based on landing-site observations, fisheries data, and interviews with fishermen. The results show that the species is a regular year-round component of artisanal catches, with an estimated annual bycatch of about 200–300 individuals. It is strongly associated with sandy continental shelf habitats and is mainly captured as bycatch in bottom-set longlines, followed by trammel nets, gillnets, and bottom trawlers, reflecting strong overlap with demersal fisheries. Total lengths ranged from approximately 60–110 cm TL, with most individuals between 70 and 100 cm TL, while body weights ranged from about 1.5 to 7 kg, with occasional larger individuals exceeding 8 kg. The population is dominated by medium-sized individuals, with relatively few large adults, suggesting possible fishing-induced demographic changes. Although not a targeted species, it is regularly retained and sold locally, contributing to small-scale economic value and food supply. These findings align with broader Mediterranean evidence of declining resilience in coastal shark populations under sustained fishing pressure. Given its role as a benthic mesopredator and vulnerability to non-selective gears, current exploitation may be unsustainable. Improved monitoring, gear selectivity, and fisher awareness are recommended to support conservation and sustainable management.
